Every job search I've done has meant 60+ applications before signing an offer. Over the years that turned into a framework I follow every time: how I prepare, how I find openings, and how I get past the filters. This is Part 1: getting interviews. Part 2 — everything from the first recruiter call to salary talk — is coming soon. The examples are Data Science/ML flavored, but most of this applies to any tech role.

## Mindset

- **It's a marathon, not a sprint.** Pace yourself for 60+ applications, not 6.
- **The first interview is the hardest step.** Most people get filtered out before ever talking to a human — there are simply too many applicants per opening. Everything below is about getting past that filter.
- **Treat it like a sales process.** In sales you always try three times. No response usually means busy, bad timing, or forgotten — not rejection. Don't be shy about reaching out to the same people again. Never take it personally.
- **Track everything.** Applications, stages, meeting notes, questions you've asked, offer details. I use Notion for this.
- **Cap your daily applications.** I schedule 3–5 per day. When I start mass applying, the quality of my applications drops drastically and it shows.

## Preparation

- Know your shit. You need a solid technical foundation for whatever the role will ask of you.
- Two books cover roughly 80% of the technical topics that come up in DS interviews, and I treat them like bibles during a job search — I keep rereading them the whole time:
  - [Ace the Data Science Interview](https://www.acethedatascienceinterview.com/)
  - [The Hundred-Page Machine Learning Book](https://themlbook.com/)
- It takes me about a week to get through everything and feel confident walking into interviews.
- Your first interviews will be rusty no matter what. Apply to jobs you care less about first; if there's somewhere you really want to work, delay that application until you have a few interviews under your belt.
- Resume: one page, single column, ATS-friendly. Summary at the top, then experience > skills > education. Every bullet should say what you did, how you did it, and the result, in data-driven impact terms.
  - There are tools online that style your resume and use LLM feedback to tell you what's not optimal and how to fix it.
  - Even better, get a professional review (salary.fyi, Fiverr) — or cheaper, ask someone with more resume-writing experience than you.

## Finding Jobs and Applying

- **Always personalize your resume to the job description. This is a must — do not skip it.** I built an n8n automation that scrapes the job description and personalizes my resume with the skills and requirements from it.
- **Speak their language.** This is the most important part of personalizing. Match your responsibilities, skills, and technologies to the job description. Don't blatantly lie about things you have zero experience with, but:
  - If they mention Supabase and you've worked with Postgres, put Supabase on the resume. A recruiter will drop you over this, because they don't know the two are practically the same thing.
  - If they're looking for someone who "solves problems consistently", write that you're a problem solver.
  - If they want data presentations to non-technical stakeholders, add a bullet to multiple jobs showing exactly that.
- Apply through the company's own site, not LinkedIn Easy Apply. If you can get a referral, do that first.
- I don't care about motivation letters and always leave them unfilled.
- **Reach out to the hiring decision makers directly.** The second most important step. On LinkedIn, search with the `Current company` filter and write to people who work there: "Hey there, saw you're looking for X, I have Y relevant experience and think I can help. Do you have 15 mins this week?"
  - Small company: CEO/CTO directly. Medium: team lead, CTO, head of tech, or technical recruiter. Big: team lead or technical recruiter.
  - Cold email works too: firstname@company.com or first.lastname@company.com often gets to them directly.
- **Follow up.** No update for 2–4 days means a short follow-up message. This is where tracking everything pays off.
- Make use of recruiters — reach out to recruiting companies and send them your resume. [This Reddit post](https://www.reddit.com/r/RemoteJobseekers/comments/1fdpeg2/how_i_landed_multiple_remote_job_offers_my_remote/) shows how to go the extra mile.
